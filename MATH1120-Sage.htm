The SIR model of epidemics

We model the spread of a disease by dividing the population into three groups:
  • S : those Susceptible to the disease, can get sick
  • I : those Infected with the disease, can spread the disease
  • R : those Recovered from the disease, are immune.

    • I'll use the normalisation: S+I+R=1S+I+R=1, i.e. these represent proportions of the population, rather than absolute numbers of people.

    The assumptions are:
  • The rate at which people get sick is proportional to the number of infected people times the number of susceptible people. This gives a term IS=I(1IR)IS=I(1IR)
  • The rate at which people recover from the disease is propotional to the number of infected people. This gives a term II.
  • Nobody dies, so S+I+RS+I+R stays 1.


    • This leads to the two differential Equations dIdt=I(1IR)IdIdt=I(1IR)I dRdt=IdRdt=I (If people lose immunity over time, then we get an additional term RR in the second DE.)

    We can solve these two linked DEs numerically using Euler's method:
    Choose a stepsize hh, start with initial values R0,I0R0,I0, then iterate: Rn+1=Rn+hdRdt(Rn,In)Rn+1=Rn+hdRdt(Rn,In) In+1=In+hdIdt(Rn,In)In+1=In+hdIdt(Rn,In) This is essentially simulating the course of the epidemic in time steps of size hh.

    One can also compute the base reproductive number R0R0 (not to be confused with R(0)R(0) ), which estimates how many people one infected individual would infect in a completely susceptible population. It's given by R0=/R0=/.

    For comparison: Flu has R01.5R01.5, SARS-CoV-19 has R02.53.5R02.53.5, and Measles has R01218.R01218. That's why Measles vaccinations are so important!

    Below, I also plot a "herd immunity threshold". For me, that's the value of SS at which dIdt=0dIdt=0, i.e. S=/S=/. When this many people are susceptible, then the rate of new infections stops increasing. If 1/1/ of the people are immune, this means that the disease won't start a new epidemic. One can achieve herd immunity by vaccinating (1/1/ ) of the population.
